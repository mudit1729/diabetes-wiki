# A1C 9.2% With CKD and Albuminuria

## Typical Patient

Type 2 diabetes, A1C about 9%, eGFR 35-60, urine ACR elevated, on metformin or no modern cardiorenal therapy, no DKA symptoms.

## First Visit Priorities

| Priority | Action |
|----------|--------|
| Confirm risk | Repeat/confirm eGFR and urine ACR; check potassium and BP. |
| Kidney foundation | ACEi/ARB if hypertension/albuminuria and tolerated; add SGLT2 inhibitor if eligible. |
| Glycemic and weight control | Add GLP-1 RA with outcome evidence if A1C/weight/ASCVD risk remains important. |
| Safety | Review sick-day rules, genital infection risk, hypoglycemia-prone drugs, and dehydration risk. |
| Follow-up | Recheck kidney function, potassium as indicated, tolerance, and home glucose in 2-6 weeks. |

## Practical Medication Direction

1. Continue metformin only if eGFR/tolerability allow.
2. Add [[wiki/medications/empagliflozin|empagliflozin]] or [[wiki/medications/dapagliflozin|dapagliflozin]] when eligible and affordable.
3. Add [[wiki/medications/semaglutide|semaglutide]] when A1C remains high, obesity/ASCVD risk is present, or FLOW-like CKD benefit is a priority.
4. Consider [[wiki/medications/finerenone|finerenone]] when albuminuria persists despite ACEi/ARB and potassium monitoring is feasible.
5. Use [[wiki/medications/basal-insulin|basal insulin]] if symptomatic/severe hyperglycemia persists or non-insulin therapy is not enough.

## Avoid

- Treating A1C alone while missing albuminuria.
- Full-dose sulfonylurea in CKD with poor meal reliability.
- Starting SGLT2 inhibitor without sick-day/perioperative education.
- Adding GLP-1 RA without reducing insulin/sulfonylurea if lows are likely.
