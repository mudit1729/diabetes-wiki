# ADA Standards of Care

## Why This Matters
The ADA Standards of Care are the main annually updated U.S. diabetes guideline set and should be the default organizing reference for diagnosis, glycemic goals, pharmacologic therapy, cardiovascular risk, kidney disease, technology, complications screening, hospital care, and pregnancy.

## Practical Domains

| Domain | Clinician Use |
|--------|---------------|
| Diagnosis/classification | Confirm diabetes type, repeat testing requirements, and prediabetes risk stratification. |
| Glycemic goals | Individualize A1C and glucose targets by life expectancy, comorbidity, hypoglycemia risk, and treatment burden. |
| Pharmacologic therapy | Choose therapy by comorbidity: ASCVD/high risk, HF, CKD, obesity, hypoglycemia risk, cost, and access. |
| Technology | Decide when CGM, pump therapy, or automated insulin delivery adds value. |
| Complications | Schedule kidney, eye, neuropathy, foot, cardiovascular, and psychosocial screening. |

## Clinical Takeaways

- **Comorbidity-first drug choice:** In type 2 diabetes with ASCVD/high risk, heart failure, or CKD, therapy selection is not only A1C-driven; cardiorenal benefit can justify SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 RA use independent of metformin status.
- **Avoid one-size A1C:** Tight control is useful early and when safe, but older adults, advanced comorbidity, hypoglycemia, and high treatment burden require relaxed goals.
- **Technology is evidence-based care:** CGM is no longer only a type 1 diabetes tool; it is relevant for insulin-treated type 2 diabetes and selected high-risk patients.
- **Screen systematically:** Diabetes care fails when kidney disease, retinopathy, neuropathy, foot risk, blood pressure, lipids, and smoking are treated as separate problems.
